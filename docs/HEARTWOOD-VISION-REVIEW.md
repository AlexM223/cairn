# Heartwood: An Independent Vision Review

**Date:** 2026-07-19
**Status:** Independent orchestrated review — an evidence sweep of the `docs/` corpus, the codebase, `git log`/`.beads/issues.jsonl` history, and three sibling reference repos, followed by two adversarial analysis passes (a skeptical-CTO acquisition framing and a product-thesis/visionary framing), synthesized here into one document. Produced at Alex's explicit request for an unflattering, non-cheerleading assessment. Every claim below is grounded in a specific file, bead ID, commit, or count from that evidence; nothing here is invented.

---

## Executive summary

Heartwood is a genuinely mature, unusually well-tested self-hosted Bitcoin wallet — single-sig, multisig, collaborative custody, five-channel fail-closed notifications — built by one person driving an AI-agent swarm over 16 calendar days (2026-07-03 to 07-19, 1,059 commits, 27 releases). Sitting on top of that stable core is a growing pile of half-built or purely-planned satellites: a C-grade mining pool that is unreachable on Umbrel, a federation epic with zero shipped code, and a coinbase-payout pool that exists entirely as paper under an undecided legal gate. This is not the usual feature bloat on a shaky foundation — it is the rarer, more treatable pattern of strategy churn on a stable base.

The one thing Heartwood actually is, underneath the marketing churn: **the always-watching, multi-user self-custody wallet that lives on your own node.** It holds keys nowhere, watches your Bitcoin and everyone you share custody with, and tells you the moment money moves — with no company anywhere in the trust path. Plain-language collaborative multisig is the expansion path inside that thesis, not a rival to it.

The top three cuts, all defensible from the project's own internal documents: kill the coinbase-payout pool epic (`cairn-l1zu`) — it carries 21 implementation beads under an unmade legal decision and reverses the project's own hard gate against reward-splitting. Demote the mining pool (`cairn-vn43`) to an off-by-default hobbyist toy — its own strategy doc admits the flywheel thesis is false until federation exists. Freeze visual identity on evergreen — this is the third proposed identity in two weeks and the first was discarded with zero surviving surface area.

The backlog is losing ground (open issues grew 3.3x in a week while total issues grew 1.7x), regression-test discipline sits at 6%, and roughly ten doctrine and legal decisions are bottlenecked on one person. None of that is a code problem.

**Update, same day:** the maintainer pushed back on this review's framing with a reframe — Heartwood as "Node Navigator," multi-user as the headline, not an expression of a watchtower thesis. The **Addendum** at the end of this document adjudicates that reframe against the same evidence base. Verdict in short: multi-user *is* under-credited above and gets promoted to co-equal billing with the watchtower; the explorer gets promoted from commodity to core surface; the "come aboard" flow is a real, currently near-empty gap the original review missed entirely. But "navigator" does not replace "watchtower" as the organizing thesis, "shared sovereign view" overclaims what the code actually enforces today, and the reframe rescues nothing for mining, the coinbase pool, or federation beyond a copy change. The merged thesis and revised sequencing are in the Addendum; the cut list is unchanged.

## What Heartwood Actually Is

Strip away the strategy documents and look at the tree. Heartwood — package name `heartwood`, version 0.2.41, MIT license, SvelteKit 2.63 / Svelte 5.56 on `adapter-node`, Node's built-in `node:sqlite` with no ORM — is a self-hosted single-sig and multisig Bitcoin wallet with an integrated block explorer, a five-channel notification system, and a bolted-on Stratum V1 solo mining pool, distributed to Umbrel-class node owners. It was built in its entirety inside a single 16-day window, 2026-07-03 through 2026-07-19, v0.1.0 to v0.2.41, 1,059 commits, 27 tagged releases — there is no "early days" to compare against a "mature phase"; everything discussed here happened inside those 16 days.

The load-bearing product is the wallet. Four subsystems carry the real mass and the real hardening, and they line up into one product rather than four:

1. **Watch-only + send single-sig wallet** (`src/lib/server/wallets.ts` 630 LOC, `walletSync.ts` 1,259 LOC, `transactions.ts` 1,221 LOC) — graded B+ in the code audit, backed by more concurrency and adversarial-input tests than anything else in the repo (`concurrencySingleSigRace.test.ts`, `sendBoundaryMatrix.test.ts` ×2, `hostileAddresses/Psbts/Xpubs.test.ts`).
2. **The PSBT/multisig crypto core** (`src/lib/server/bitcoin/`, 13,495 LOC across 27 files) — near 1:1 test-to-code ratio (`psbt.ts` 1,301 LOC vs `psbt.test.ts` 1,611 LOC; `multisigPsbt.ts` 995 LOC vs its 1,260-LOC test), adversarial-input suites (`malformedSendInputs.test.ts` 583 LOC, `hostilePsbts.test.ts` 386 LOC, `hugeUtxoSet.test.ts` 254 LOC), zero P0 findings in the 2026-07-05 security audit.
3. **Five-channel notification system** (email/Telegram/ntfy/Nostr/webhook, `src/lib/server/channels/`, 3,460 LOC across 15 files) driven by SPV-verified payment detection that is explicitly documented as one of MANUAL.md's five non-negotiable invariants: fails closed.
4. **A chain stack with no third-party dependency** — Bitcoin Core RPC (`bitcoinCore/client.ts`, 411 LOC) as the canonical source since the deliberate v0.2.9 Esplora removal, Electrum (`electrum/client.ts`, 760 LOC + `pool.ts`) as a second rail.

Everything past that is a different maturity class. The block explorer (`chain/index.ts`, 2,054 LOC — the single largest non-test file in the repo, plus a 2,175-LOC test file) is real and large but is feature-flagged off by default for new installs, and per standing notes is "pool-blind" (`cairn-r1hca`) despite its size. The mining pool (`src/lib/server/mining/`, ~5,951 LOC across 24 files) is graded C: it is a genuinely substantial, well-documented Stratum V1 server, but it shipped in the same wave as a P1 that silently blanked the entire `/mining` dashboard in production (`cairn-et5a0`, a duplicate `{height}:{vout}` key in a Svelte keyed-each causing a hydration crash, fixed one day before this review in commit `88868eb`). Federation (`cairn-cz3q`) and the coinbase-payout pool (`cairn-l1zu`) are, respectively, zero-shipped-code and pure-paper.

The honest one-line description: a competent, disciplined self-custody wallet with an outsized pile of undecided, researched-but-unbuilt strategy sitting on top of it. This is not feature bloat on a shaky base — the foundation is the least volatile part of the project. It is strategy churn on a stable base, and that is a materially different (and more fixable) problem.

## Real Strengths

**The PSBT/crypto core is the crown jewel.** 13,495 LOC in `lib/server/bitcoin/` where the test files outweigh the implementation files in the money-handling paths. This is the hardest part of a Bitcoin wallet to get right, and it is the part that is most right. The 2026-07-05 security audit found five real P1s and zero P0s, every P1 gated behind a real-world precondition (MITM, malicious signing device, social engineering) — a genuinely reassuring result for a self-custody product.

**Test discipline is real and rare for a solo project.** 290 `*.test.ts` files, roughly 62,000 LOC, about a third of the codebase by line count, with six separate `concurrency*.test.ts` files (multi-handle, multisig race, single-sig race, write burst, concurrent logins, concurrent sessions) — the kind of coverage that typically only gets written after real production incidents, meaning the pain was felt and answered rather than theorized about.

**Non-custodial invariants are enforced in code, not just policy.** The mining engine hard-errors in `job.ts` if more than one value-bearing coinbase output would exist — the `cairn-vn43.14` gate against reward-splitting is backed by an executable assertion, not a paragraph in a design doc. That is one of the few places in the corpus where doctrine and code cannot drift apart silently.

**The self-correction and honesty discipline is the most unusual asset in the whole project.** The internal docs repeatedly undercut the project's own marketing. `HASH-ATTRACTION-STRATEGY.md` states outright that the "more users → more hash → more attractive" flywheel narrative is false at the instance level (each Heartwood instance is a separate pool; hash does not combine before federation exists) and quantifies the discomfort plainly: 1 TH/s earns roughly $11.3/yr in expected value at any pool size, and small instances have a 73% chance of earning zero fee revenue after 100 years. `COINBASE-POOL-ANALYSIS.md` states plainly that small operators will almost certainly never earn fee revenue. `COMPETITOR-ANALYSIS-R3-2026-07-19.md` walks back the "only multi-user solo pool with per-connection coinbase" uniqueness claim once it found that Public Pool already ships the identical mechanic at 0% fee with real confirmed block finds. This candor is worth a real premium in any external assessment — the internal record does not read as a pitch deck lying to itself.

**No third party sits in the trust path.** Esplora was deliberately removed in v0.2.9; the app now talks directly to the user's own Bitcoin Core plus Electrum. Combined with the notification system, this produces something the sibling repos (Bastion, Citadel/NodeView) don't have: an active wallet that pushes state to the user rather than one they have to remember to open and check.

**Plain-language, guided-wizard UX is doctrine, not an afterthought.** The refs survey against the author's own prior repos (Citadel/NodeView, then Bastion) shows this same author has built the node-as-a-service shape twice already. What Heartwood does differently is layer no-Bitcoin-internals-exposed, guided-wizard UX on top of a correct core — Caravan round-trip import/export, four hardware-wallet vendors, per-key health tracking (`keyHealth.ts`). That is the one thing in the portfolio that is both good and not already reproven by a sibling repo.

**Narrow epics close.** `cairn-ivae` (admin comparison) is 7/7 closed. `cairn-6efi` (explorer redesign) is 12/14 closed. `cairn-vn43` (mining pool, as actually shipped) is 16/24 closed — the healthiest of the six named epics by a wide margin over the two that are churning.

## Real Weaknesses

**Single-sig and multisig is a near-total parallel implementation.** Two route trees (`/wallets/*` and `/wallets/multisig/*`), two API mirrors, two server-module families (`wallets.ts`/`transactions.ts` versus `multisig.ts`, `multisigPsbt.ts`, `multisigTransactions.ts`, `multisigShares.ts`, `multisigRoster.ts`, `multisigRegistrations.ts`, `multisigExport.ts` — roughly 3,940 LOC of multisig server logic mirroring the single-sig side), two test suites (`wallets-multisig.test.ts` 889 LOC alongside `wallets.test.ts` 446 LOC). Roughly 1,500–2,000 duplicated lines in total. The most dangerous instance: the RBF/CPFP broadcast-claim logic — the most dangerous line in the codebase to have two of — is duplicated between the two paths. Tracked since 2026-07-06 as `cairn-rg99` and still open. It doubles the bug surface of the core money-moving path indefinitely until fixed.

**Dual chain-data backends were never consolidated.** Electrum (`electrum/`, 2,279 LOC) and Bitcoin Core RPC (`bitcoinCore/`, 755 LOC) are two independently-implemented clients that must stay behaviorally reconciled since the v0.2.9 Esplora removal made Core canonical but never retired Electrum as a second rail — a permanent maintenance tax on every chain-data change.

**The `lib/server/` root is roughly 150 files with no folder boundaries.** Auth, wallets, transactions, notifications-core, admin, backups, activity, and recovery all sit loose at the top level of one directory. Individually the files are usually well-scoped and well-tested; collectively it is the debt of a codebase that grew faster than it was organized.

**Recurring bug families prove that fixes are addressing instances, not root causes.** Four distinct beads across two separate audit waves describe the same underlying failure — coinbase/UTXO state not modeled as a first-class distinct case: `cairn-8lwa6` (immature coinbase silently shown as spendable), `cairn-e176o` (spendable total never subtracts spent coinbase), `cairn-25ges` (home total silently includes immature coinbase), `cairn-i0d0q` (mining payouts render as generic "Received," no reward identity). The first pass fixed instances, not the pattern. Separately, `cairn-et5a0` silently blanked the production `/mining` dashboard via a hydration crash, surviving an entire release wave (v0.2.34–v0.2.37) undetected until a retrospective audit caught it.

**Regression-test discipline is a named, proven-non-theoretical gap.** The 2026-07-06 process retrospective, analyzing 305 closed beads, found that only 6% of bug fixes added a regression test — and documents a case of the same bug being reintroduced almost verbatim eleven hours after being fixed, in new code, by a different session. `fix:`-prefixed commits (328) outnumber `feat:`-prefixed commits (236) by 39%, roughly one in three commits in a 16-day-old codebase being a fix rather than new capability.

**The backlog is measurably losing ground.** The 2026-07-12 `BEADS-AUDIT.md` snapshot recorded 65 open of 648 total issues. Seven days later, on 2026-07-19: 216 open of 1,124 total. Open issues grew 3.3x while total issues grew only 1.7x — filing is outrunning closing, and the gap is widening, not narrowing. Today alone produced 109 filed against 39 closed, the sharpest single-day net-growth on record. Current state: 3 open P0s, 44 open P1s, 70 open P2s, 99 open P3s.

The three open P0s are: `cairn-l1zu.1` (the coinbase-payout-pool legal gate), `cairn-cz3q.4` (an unresolved umbrelOS onion-address verification spike blocking federation), and `cairn-cz3q.11` (cosigner-side independent PSBT verification, still at design stage). Of the 44 open P1s, 18 belong to federation and 10 belong to the coinbase-payout pool — 28 of 44 open P1s sit inside the two epics that are more than 90% unbuilt.

**Doctrine and legal decisions are bottlenecked on one person.** Counting distinct "blocked on Alex" items across the evidence: the mining-payout legal gate (P0), the federation onion-availability spike, at least seven design/UX doctrine calls (sats-display threshold, USD-default reversal, amber/red degraded-state taxonomy, non-color accessibility redundancy, a reassurance-copy carve-out, a net-new feature go/no-go, sovereignty-honesty doctrine), and the Umbrel App-Store identity question (`cairn-koy4.13`, unresolved 12 days). `HEARTWOOD-ROADMAP.md`'s own decision-gate table lists ten items, all owned by Alex — a structural throughput ceiling no additional AI-agent research capacity relieves.

**Three visual identities in two weeks.** Copper/hairline tokens (`cairn-koy4`, shipped v0.1.9, 2026-07-06/07) were fully superseded by evergreen-ink (`cairn-sdx5`, shipped v0.2.25, 2026-07-14/15) — the koy4 close comment concedes it outright: "supersedes koy4 copper." A third wave of proposed, explicitly unratified amendments landed 2026-07-19, including a proposal to reverse the sats-first display default the second identity had just established as its single most load-bearing rule (`cairn-4vh2a`). The first identity produced zero surviving surface area.

**The strategy-doc production machine is outrunning the decision loop.** Roughly 65 files in `docs/`, several 30–80KB, produced in about two weeks by one human's worth of review bandwidth. Mining strategy alone received three full treatments in six days (`MINING-POOL-SCOPE.md` → `COINBASE-PAYOUT-POOL-DESIGN.md` + `COINBASE-POOL-ANALYSIS.md` → `HASH-ATTRACTION-STRATEGY.md`) against only 21 commits (about 2% of project history) of actual mining code.

**The tracker itself is a meaningful cost center.** 362 of 1,059 commits (34%) touch `.beads/issues.jsonl` — more commits reference the issue tracker than touch the wallet, the actual product surface (159 commits, about 15%).

**A known privacy leak persists.** The 2026-07-06 data audit found that a read-only "viewer" share on a shared multisig wallet can see the full raw PSBT — including recipient addresses and amounts — of in-flight draft transactions, and can even edit shared address labels, despite the product's stated intent that viewers should see strictly less than cosigners.

## The Honest Ambitious Vision

Start from base rates: single maintainer, 16 days old, self-hosted Bitcoin wallet category, Umbrel distribution. In that context, "become the sovereign Bitcoin commerce network" (Tessera's actual thesis, an eight-pillar vision gated on a testnet4 block that has never been found and paid) is fantasy for this project. So is "win as a pool" — the project's own `HASH-ATTRACTION-STRATEGY.md` already conceded that on 2026-07-19.

Both completed analyses converge, from different starting framings, on the same core thesis:

**Heartwood is the always-watching, multi-user self-custody wallet that lives on your own node: it holds keys nowhere, watches your Bitcoin and everyone you share custody with, and tells you the moment money moves — with no company anywhere in the trust path.**

Push, not pull. Sovereign, not relayed. The evidence for this being the true center of gravity, not a marketing reframe: the code's largest and best-tested investments are exactly the four subsystems that build this — single-sig wallet, PSBT/multisig core, five-channel fail-closed notifications, and the no-third-party chain stack. No other candidate thesis is backed by comparable code mass, and no other candidate thesis survives contact with the project's own internal audits.

Collaborative multisig — a non-expert able to complete the multisig wizard, four hardware vendors, Caravan round-trip — is the expansion path *inside* that thesis, not a rival to it: it is "the people you trust" made concrete. This is the CTO analysis's pick for the sharpest available wedge, and it is correct, but it should be understood as one expression of the watchtower thesis rather than a separate bet competing with it for investment.

### Rejected theses, and why

| Thesis | Verdict | Why rejected |
|---|---|---|
| "Solo mining with friends" | Reject | The project's own `HASH-ATTRACTION-STRATEGY.md` states the flywheel is false at the instance level; Public Pool already ships the identical multi-user-solo, per-connection-coinbase mechanic at 0% fee with real confirmed block finds; the economics require roughly 1 PH/s to matter, a scale no self-hosted instance reaches; 1 TH/s earns about $11.3/yr in expected value regardless of pool size. |
| "Family bank on your node" | Reject (overclaims) | No spending controls, spend limits, or approval-threshold page exists anywhere in the product. The notification and multi-user machinery gestures at this shape, but the control surface a "bank" implies is not built. |
| "Self-hosted Bitcoin home screen" | Reject (wrong altitude) | This is already Bastion and Citadel/NodeView — the same author has built this exact node-as-a-service shape twice before, at higher feature maturity in Bastion's case (federation, mining, social layer, guided UX all further along). Heartwood re-proving that shape works is redundant effort against its own prior art. |
| Always-watching, no-custodian, multi-user watchtower wallet | **Accept — the thesis** | Backed by the best-built, best-tested, most-invested code in the repo; the only thesis none of the project's own self-critical docs contradict; turns the five-channel notification investment into the headline behavior (push, not pull) instead of a side feature. |

## What To Cut

**Kill the coinbase-payout pool epic (`cairn-l1zu`).** This is the clearest cut available. Twenty-one implementation beads — `types.ts`, DB migrations, a multi-output coinbase builder in `job.ts`, a `stratum.ts` payout-set rework, `leaderboard.ts`, settings surface, auth lookup, a QA gate, plus three open product-parameter decisions (finder percentage, pot distribution, sybil-resistance minimum) — are written underneath a still-open P0 legal gate (`l1zu.1`, titled verbatim "decide the `cairn-vn43.14` reversal"). That gate exists because `MINING-POOL-SCOPE.md` (2026-07-13) drew a hard line: "Raffle layer dropped. Operator-run prize draws for third parties = gambling... any future multi-user or shared-reward mode requires legal review before any code." `l1zu` proposes exactly the mode that line was drawn to prevent. The economics only work above roughly 1–10 PH/s, a scale no self-hosted instance reaches. It reverses the project's own no-custody promise — one user's reward output being split by the operator is a form of custody the rest of the product goes out of its way to avoid. Keep the analysis docs as reference; do not write another implementation bead until counsel actually clears `l1zu.1`, which requires Alex to send the counsel package that already exists (`COUNSEL-QUESTION-PACKAGE-L1ZU1-2026-07-19.md`) and, as of this review, has not been sent.

**Demote the mining pool (`cairn-vn43`) to an off-by-default hobbyist toy.** It shipped — 16 of 24 children closed, the healthiest of the flagship epics by completion rate — so it is sunk cost worth keeping behind its existing feature flag. But its own strategy document admits the thesis is false until federation exists, it is currently unreachable on Umbrel at all (`cairn-kgj7a`: stratum ports not published in the Umbrel docker-compose), it shipped a silent prod-blanking P1 (`et5a0`) that survived a full release wave undetected, and a direct competitor (Public Pool) already ships the identical mechanic with real block finds. Stop investing. Fix reachability if the flag stays on at all, then freeze.

**Freeze visual identity on evergreen.** Ratify `DESIGN-MANIFESTO.md` as-is. Do not act on the 2026-07-19 unratified-amendments wave beyond what is already load-bearing; explicitly do not reverse sats-first display (`cairn-4vh2a`) without a real decision process, and do not produce a third full token/component pass. The first identity (copper) produced zero surviving surface area after eight days; a third rebrand in as many weeks is the risk to pre-empt now, not discover later.

**Halt the strategy-doc production machine until the open decision queue clears.** No new 30–80KB research documents while roughly ten doctrine and legal decisions sit unmade on one person's desk. This does not mean stop thinking — the self-correction discipline visible in the corpus is a real strength — it means stop manufacturing analysis faster than it can be decided and shipped.

**Park federation (`cairn-cz3q`), do not cut it.** Unlike the payout pool, federation reinforces the core thesis — collaborative custody across households and trusted friends is a direct extension of "the people you trust." But it is 29 of 31 children open, the largest all-open epic in the tracker, gated on an unresolved ship-blocker spike (`cz3q.4`, whether umbrelOS still exposes per-app onion addresses) with zero shipped code and zero closed-loop validation. Keep the design (it correctly adopts Bastion's proven parts — ed25519 instance identity, fingerprint-only key invites — and fixes two of Bastion's named structural gaps: the open-handshake DoS hole and the disconnected local/remote cosigning split) as a spec on the shelf. Sequence it as the honest phase-two expansion of collaborative custody, after single-instance multisig UX and the wallet consolidation below are actually done — not now.

**The difficulty raffle stays dead.** `DIFFICULTY-RAFFLE-ANALYSIS.md` (2026-07-17) already concluded best-share-wins is analytically superior to the old HMAC-PRNG raffle design as a mechanism, but that the legal gate is about whether reward gets redistributed, not how a winner is chosen — so the same `vn43.14` gate applies regardless. No further analysis needed here; this one is already correctly settled.

**Net effect:** removing `l1zu` and freezing `vn43`/identity/the doc machine collapses the ten-item decision-gate table down to the handful that actually gate the wallet, directly attacking the single-maintainer bottleneck that is the real constraint on this project.

## Where Effort Isn't Compounding

The evidence quantifies this cleanly, and the pattern is consistent across every measure:

- **Three mining strategy passes and three UX-psychology research rounds against one shipped, feature-flagged-off, C-grade pool feature.** `MINING-POOL-SCOPE.md` → `COINBASE-PAYOUT-POOL-DESIGN.md`/`COINBASE-POOL-ANALYSIS.md` → `HASH-ATTRACTION-STRATEGY.md`, six days of document time, against 21 commits (roughly 2%) of actual mining code across the project's history. The UX-psychology research line (R1 2026-07-15, R2 2026-07-18, R3 2026-07-19) is genuinely strong, well-cited work, but by R3 it has produced at least seven distinct still-pending doctrine decisions rather than converging.
- **362 of 1,059 commits (34%) touch the issue tracker** — more commits reference `.beads/issues.jsonl` than touch the wallet itself (159 commits, ~15%). Today's single day produced 109 new issues against 39 closed, the largest net-backlog-growth day on record.
- **Two full visual-identity systems built in eight days, one with zero surviving surface area.** Copper (`cairn-koy4`, shipped v0.1.9, 2026-07-06/07) was fully closed and replaced by evergreen (`cairn-sdx5`, shipped v0.2.25, 2026-07-14/15). Meanwhile the one identity decision that actually matters to existing installs — whether the Umbrel App-Store ID or the `CAIRN_DB` environment variable ever changes (`cairn-koy4.13`) — has sat unresolved for 12 days, untouched by either rebrand.
- **Twenty-one implementation beads written under an unmade legal decision** (`l1zu`, detailed above) — by the CTO analysis's own reasoning, effort 100% at risk of full discard depending on how `l1zu.1` resolves.
- **Federation has real design depth and zero shipped code.** Ed25519 identity, Tor transport, unified local/remote cosigning, PSBT session endpoints — 29 of 31 beads open, the largest all-open epic in the tracker, with no code, no user, and no learning fed back into the design yet.
- **Fix commits (328) outnumber feat commits (236) by 39%** in a 16-day-old codebase — a high fix ratio for a project this young, consistent with the 6% regression-test rate meaning many fixes don't stay fixed the first time.
- **A known-broken epic sat unfixed for at least seven days after diagnosis.** `cairn-6xxa` (a performance re-architecture epic) was flagged in the 2026-07-12 `BEADS-AUDIT.md` as "inverted and empty" — zero content on the parent, six real perf beads carrying blocks-edges on it and therefore unable to surface as ready work. As of this review it is still open with no evidence the wiring was fixed. Filed, diagnosed, never resolved — a small but exact microcosm of the whole pattern.

By contrast, what compounds is uniform in shape: `cairn-ivae` (7/7 closed), `cairn-6efi` (12/14 closed), `cairn-vn43` (16/24 closed) all answered their single hardest question once, up front, then executed against a bounded scope. `cairn-l1zu` and `cairn-cz3q` both wrote implementation beads while their hardest question — a legal decision, an infrastructure spike — was still open. That contrast is the single most actionable finding in the entire evidence set.

## The Next Three Moves

**1. Make the watchtower unimpeachable.** The thesis only holds if the wallet-that-watches genuinely cannot fail silently. That means: verify notification deep-links land correctly and payment detection truly fails closed under every failure mode (a recurring P1 across multiple prior ship cycles); close the viewer-sees-full-PSBT least-privilege leak from the 2026-07-06 data audit; and fix the immature-coinbase bug family at its root — `cairn-8lwa6`, `cairn-e176o`, `cairn-25ges`, and `cairn-i0d0q` are one bug, not four, and should be closed as one root-cause fix rather than four instance patches. Every fix in this pass gets a regression test, no exceptions — this is the single highest-leverage process change available given the 6% baseline.

**2. Land UX redesign phases 2–4 and clear the decision queue.** `UX-REDESIGN-SPEC.md`'s Phase 1 (Home) is shipped; Phases 2–4 (Send, Settings/Health, Nav+Explorer) are still open under epic `cairn-gt05` (9 of 12 children still open despite real UX-psychology work having already landed outside the epic's own numbering). In parallel, batch-ratify the roughly ten parked doctrine calls in one sitting — sats/BTC display threshold, USD-default, amber/red taxonomy, accessibility redundancy, the reassurance-copy carve-out, the net-new feature go/no-go, the Umbrel App-Store identity question — rather than letting them accumulate one research round at a time. Freeze identity for the duration; do not let a UX pass reopen the rebrand question.

**3. Consolidate the wallet, then earn federation.** Merge single-sig and multisig starting with the broadcast path — `cairn-rg99`, open since 2026-07-06, is the highest-value fix in the codebase because it is the one duplication with direct money-movement risk. Retire one of the two chain-data backends (Electrum or Core RPC as the sole rail, with the other demoted to an explicit fallback rather than a permanently-parallel client). Only once the wallet core is one implementation instead of two should federation begin — at that point it is the honest, low-risk expansion of "the people you trust," built against Bastion's already-audited protocol shape rather than from scratch.

## Process Findings

The clearest process signal in the whole evidence set is a clean split: epics that answered their hardest question before writing a single implementation bead closed, and epics that wrote implementation beads while the hardest question was still open did not. `cairn-ivae`, `cairn-6efi`, and `cairn-vn43` all had one coherent architectural or scope decision made once, up front, then executed against a bounded target. `cairn-l1zu` and `cairn-cz3q` both accumulated 20-plus implementation-shaped beads — file names, function signatures, migration plans — while a P0 legal gate and a P0 infrastructure spike, respectively, remained unresolved underneath them. This is not a subtle correlation; it is the same pattern twice, in the project's two largest and least healthy epics.

The single-maintainer decision bottleneck is the real throughput ceiling, not engineering capacity. The AI-agent swarm can produce documentation, code, and analysis far faster than one person can review and decide — and the evidence shows it doing exactly that: 65 files in `docs/`, several 30–80KB, roughly ten live decision gates, a strategy-doc corpus that by its own internal admission has "outrun the one human decision that would make any of it actionable" (the coinbase-pool legal gate having sat open since at least 2026-07-13, called "the fastest unblock on the whole roadmap" by the roadmap document itself, and still unactioned five-plus days later).

The self-correction discipline visible throughout the docs — mining sidecar reversed to in-process, e-cash federation abandoned for sovereign PSBT federation, equal-split payout defaults corrected to work-proportional, a uniqueness claim walked back after finding Public Pool — is real and rare, and should not be mistaken for the churn problem. The corrections are honest. What they don't do is shrink scope: each pass adds new analysis, new edge cases, new decision gates, rather than cutting a feature down to something that ships. Self-correction without scope reduction is why the project can be simultaneously unusually honest with itself and still accumulating unresolved strategic surface faster than it resolves it.

The 6% regression-test rate is where effort stops compounding at the smallest, most concrete scale. A fix that doesn't get a regression test is a fix that has some real probability of being silently undone — the corpus documents this happening literally, a bug reintroduced almost verbatim eleven hours after being fixed, by a different session, in new code. Multiply that across 328 fix commits and the aggregate risk is not hypothetical. This is the cheapest of the process fixes available: it does not require Alex to make a single new decision, only to hold the existing beads-for-every-fix discipline to a stricter bar.

## Addendum (2026-07-19): The Node Navigator Reframe

### The reframe, stated fairly

Same day this review shipped, Alex pushed back on it directly: Heartwood is not a wallet and not an explorer — it's a **Node Navigator**. A captain runs the node. They invite family, friends, or a small community — the crew — to join. Crew members don't need their own node, their own Electrum server, their own chain sync; they get the captain's verified chain, the captain's mempool, the captain's pool, all through one shared view. The differentiator, in this framing, isn't the watchtower — it's that Heartwood is the only product in the category where you invite someone *into your node's world*. Sparrow and BlueWallet are solo instruments; Nunchuk shares keys but not the experience of running a node. Under this reading, multi-user stops being one feature among several and becomes the organizing idea; invite and onboarding become the most important surface in the product; mining reframes as "the crew mines together"; federation reframes as "connecting captains."

This review takes that reframe seriously and tests it against the same evidence base used above — code, beads, commit history — rather than against the story on its own terms. Two adversarial passes were run against it (a steelman pass built to find everything the reframe gets right, and an attack pass built to find where it overclaims or changes nothing), both grounded in file-level code checks, then adjudicated here. Some of it survives. Some of it doesn't.

### What survives

**Multi-user as THE differentiator, not a side feature — adopted.** This is real and code-backed, not aspirational. Team mode, the full invite lifecycle (admin-side creation with labels and expiry, revoke, `?invite=CODE` prefill per `cairn-h3x6`, `redeemInvite` with admin notification, dedicated lifecycle tests), `multisigShares`, and a real three-tier owner/cosigner/viewer role model are all shipped, not planned. Checked directly against the sibling-repo evidence: none of Sparrow, BlueWallet, Nunchuk, or Bastion offer "invite someone into your node's world" with plain-language, no-node-of-their-own UX. The original review under-weighted this — it treated collaborative multisig as an expression of the watchtower thesis rather than crediting it as a comparably load-bearing investment in its own right. That correction stands.

**The explorer, promoted from commodity to core surface.** The original review (§"What Heartwood Actually Is") filed the explorer as feature-flagged-off, pool-blind, real-but-secondary. Under the navigator reading it's something else entirely: the shared instrument panel, the organ through which the crew actually sees the captain's chain. It is already the largest non-test subsystem in the codebase (`chain/index.ts`, 2,054 LOC, plus a 2,175-LOC test file) — "this is the biggest thing you built almost by accident, now build the story it belongs to" is better capital allocation advice than "it's a commodity, why is it so big." This reframing justifies two concrete things: fixing explorer pool-blindness (`cairn-r1hca`) stops being a nice-to-have and becomes core-surface hygiene, and the explorer's off-by-default status is worth revisiting once the surface matures — not flipping it today, but no longer treating "commodity" as the last word on it.

**The "come aboard" flow as a real gap the original review missed.** This is the sharpest thing the reframe surfaces. Graded directly against the code: invite *plumbing* is a solid B-. `/admin/invites` supports create/label/expiry/revoke; the signup page prefills the invite code from a `?invite=` link; `redeemInvite` notifies the admin; there's real lifecycle test coverage. But the invite *experience* — the actual page a crew member lands on — is an F. `src/routes/(auth)/signup/+page.svelte` is a generic SaaS signup form: display name, email, password, one invite-code field with a single hint line. There is no captain identity anywhere on it (no "you've been invited to Alex's node"), no preview of what the crew member is about to see, no guided first-run distinguishing "you're joining someone's node" from "you're setting up your own." If multi-user really is the differentiator, this is the flagship surface, and today it has almost none of the product built for it. This is a genuine finding, not a restatement of something already in the review.

**The crew-social reading of already-shipped surfaces.** The best-share leaderboard and public pool stats (`c3d9458`) and the "You found this block" explorer banner (`c9c4cf1`) sit oddly under the original review's solo-watchtower thesis — they're social features bolted onto a push-notification product. Under the navigator reading they make immediate sense as crew scoreboards: the shared thing the crew brags about together. They're already built; wiring them into the come-aboard flow (see Move 2 below) costs close to nothing.

### What doesn't survive

**"Navigator" as the organizing thesis — rejected.** It's a pull-verb metaphor sitting on top of a push product. The single best-built, most-invested piece of engineering in the repo is the five-channel, fail-closed notification machine (~3,460 LOC) that runs whether or not anyone opens the app — that is watchtower behavior, not navigation behavior. More concretely: the navigator story is silent on every hard question this review actually turned on — `rg99` (wallet-dedup), `l1zu.1` (legal gate), backend consolidation, identity ratification. A thesis that reprioritizes nothing is a slogan, not a strategy. The watchtower stays the spine; the navigator framing is real, but it describes the social layer sitting on top of that spine, not a replacement for it.

**"Everyone shares the same sovereign view" — rejected as stated.** The code doesn't support the claim as written. Read access to a shared multisig wallet is gated by `wallets/multisig.ts:174` — "Owner OR any accepted share (viewer or cosigner)" — meaning the crew's view is served *from the captain's database* and *filtered by the captain's role grants*, not some independent shared vantage point. Worse, the standing known leak (2026-07-06 data audit, cited above in Real Weaknesses) means a read-only viewer can currently see the full raw PSBT of an in-flight draft, including recipient addresses and amounts — least-privilege is not actually enforced today. Crew members are sovereign over their *keys* — the server holds none, and that is real and enforced. They are not sovereign over their *view* — that is delegated trust in whoever runs the node. The accurate claim is "your keys are yours; the view is hosted by your captain," which is a fine story but a different one. Shipping "shared sovereign view" as a headline before the PSBT leak is closed is building the front door before fixing the lock.

**Mining re-inflation — rejected.** Nothing about the crew framing changes a number. The flywheel is still false at the instance level, 1 TH/s still nets about $11.3/yr in expected value, small instances still have a 73% chance of zero fee revenue after 100 years, Public Pool still ships the identical multi-user-solo mechanic with real block finds, stratum is still unreachable by default on Umbrel (`cairn-kgj7a`), and the prod dashboard still blanked silently for a full release wave (`cairn-et5a0`). The one thing the social frame legitimately rescues: mining as an off-by-default, honestly-labeled shared ritual — near-misses and leaderboards are fine, earnings projection is not (`UX-PSYCHOLOGY-NOTES.md` already drew this exact line), and the `vn43.14` guard enforcing a single value-bearing coinbase output is the mechanism that keeps it honest. The demotion in the main review stands unchanged: no new mining investment, wire the existing social surfaces (leaderboard, found-block banner) into the come-aboard story since they're already built, and only fix `kgj7a` reachability if the flag ships on at all.

**`l1zu` rescue attempt — rejected, harder than before.** "The crew shares the catch" is a friendlier sentence for exactly the reward-splitting the project's own hard gate (`MINING-POOL-SCOPE.md`) forbade without legal review. If anything, the trust-the-captain framing makes an operator-split coinbase *more* of a betrayal, not less — the crew is now explicitly trusting the captain not to touch their money, and `l1zu` proposes the captain touch it. The kill stands. Legal gate `l1zu.1` remains unsent.

**Federation-start-now — rejected.** "Connecting captains" is a better sentence for federation than anything in the original review, but the readiness facts underneath it are unchanged: zero shipped code, 29 of 31 beads open, the `cz3q.4` onion-address spike still unresolved. A sharper story doesn't change what's actually buildable this week. Still parked, still phase two.

### The merged thesis

*Heartwood is the always-watching wallet for the people you trust: a captain runs the node and invites the crew into its world — one verified chain, one mempool, one shared view — while every member keeps their own keys, and the watchtower tells each of them the moment their money moves. Keys are sovereign; the view is the captain's hospitality; nothing leaves the node.*

### Revised sequencing (replaces the original Next Three Moves — now four)

1. **Unchanged, and elevated.** Make the watchtower unimpeachable: notification fail-closed and deep-link correctness, the immature-coinbase root cause. The viewer-sees-full-PSBT least-privilege leak is no longer routine hygiene — it is now a *prerequisite* for any "shared view" marketing claim, because the navigator story makes a promise about that view that the code doesn't yet keep.
2. **New.** Build the "come aboard" flagship: a captain-identified invite landing page, an onboarding sequence that shows the shared view — explorer, crew scoreboards — *before* asking for keys, and a discoverable team-mode toggle instead of a buried setting. Fix `cairn-r1hca` so the shared view a new crew member lands on isn't pool-blind. Note: flipping the `instanceMode` default from `'solo'` (`settings.ts:13`; `instanceModeMigration.ts` only flips to team mode on evidence of prior multi-user use) is a doctrine call, not an engineering one — add it to Alex's decision queue rather than assuming it as part of this move.
3. UX redesign phases 2–4, plus batch-ratifying the parked decision queue — now including the solo-default question above — plus the identity freeze, unchanged from the original review.
4. Consolidate the wallet (`rg99`, one broadcast path), then earn federation as "connecting captains" once `cz3q.4` clears — same sequencing as before, restated in the navigator's own language.

The cut list from the main review is unchanged in full: kill `l1zu`, demote mining, freeze identity on evergreen, halt new strategy docs until the decision queue clears, park federation.

### The meta-risk

The reframe arrived as re-interpretation, not new evidence — every code fact it leans on (team mode, invite lifecycle, the explorer's size, the leaderboard, the PSBT leak) was already sitting in the tree this review was written from, hours earlier. "Mining makes more sense now" and "federation becomes connecting captains" are the same rhetorical move: a metaphor that makes an unresolved bet sound more resolved without adding a line of code or a legal opinion. That move pattern-matches the strategy-churn signature this review already documented independently — three mining strategy passes, two visual identities in eight days with the first fully discarded, beads written before the decisions underneath them were made. The standing rule for the navigator era: story elevation must not become build-list expansion. The navigator framing earns headline status in the project's own docs only after Move 2 above actually ships — not before.
